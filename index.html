Forward Look

What to Expect Next

  • With the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed and multiple nations releasing strategic oil reserves, analysts expect continued upward pressure on global energy prices and potential fuel rationing measures in import-dependent countries, as already seen in the Philippines and Pakistan. Historically, prolonged disruptions to Gulf shipping lanes have triggered sustained economic consequences well beyond the region.
  • Iran's declared strategy of "war of attrition on several fronts" — targeting Gulf infrastructure and maintaining pressure through proxies — suggests the conflict may widen rather than contract in the near term. Observers are watching whether Yemen-based Houthis will reopen hostilities near the Red Sea's Bab el-Mandeb strait, which would further constrict global shipping.
  • Lebanon's call for ceasefire talks with Israel, combined with the growing displacement crisis exceeding 700,000 people, historically parallels diplomatic openings seen during the 2006 Lebanon war. However, analysts note that simultaneous Israeli escalation in central Beirut complicates prospects for near-term de-escalation on the Lebanese front.
  • Growing domestic opposition in the US, alongside allied governments questioning the legality and scope of the strikes — as seen with Australian Labor MPs and Spanish diplomatic action — may increase political pressure on the Trump administration to articulate a defined endgame. Historically, declining public support has constrained US military campaigns, though the timeline for policy impact varies significantly.
  • Reports of Russia assisting Iran's military suggest the conflict could become a broader geopolitical proxy confrontation. Analysts expect diplomatic activity at the UN Security Council to intensify, though historically, vetoes by permanent members have limited the body's ability to mandate ceasefires in conflicts involving major powers.
